Why the Sticker Price on Diapers and Formula Is Almost Always Misleading

The biggest pack isn't always the cheapest one, and the "sale" tag isn't either. Here's how we calculate real unit price, and how to sanity-check it yourself in about ten seconds.

By the BabyPrices team · Last updated July 13, 2026

The trap: comparing totals instead of units

Two diaper packs sitting next to each other on Amazon can look like a straightforward choice — $34.99 versus $44.94 — until you notice one box has 216 diapers and the other has 144. The "expensive" box is actually the better deal per diaper. Retailers aren't hiding this on purpose; it's just that pack sizes vary enough between brands that your brain can't do the division while scrolling. That's the entire reason this site exists.

Luvs, 216 ct, $34.99 → $34.99 ÷ 216 = $0.162 per diaper cheaper
Pampers, 144 ct, $44.94 → $44.94 ÷ 144 = $0.312 per diaper

Same size, same general purpose, nearly 2× difference once you divide it out. Neither price is wrong or deceptive — you just have to do the division to see it, which is the one step most people skip while shopping on a phone.

Formula is trickier: powder weight isn't feeding volume

Diapers are simple — count divided by price. Formula has a hidden step: the ounces printed on a tub are ounces of powder, not ounces of prepared bottle. A tub of powder gets mixed with water and yields several times its own weight in ready-to-feed formula. That's why we calculate and compare formula by price-per-ounce-of-powder rather than price-per-fluid-ounce-consumed — it's the number that actually matches what's printed on the container and what you're paying for, and it stays comparable across brands even though mixing ratios shift slightly from one to the next.

Three things worth watching as your baby grows

  • Sizes change the math. A "Size 4" pack today might be a "Size 5" pack in two months, and larger sizes almost always cost more per diaper — fewer fit in a similarly sized box. Re-check unit price when you size up rather than assuming your favorite brand is still the cheapest option at the new size.
  • Bigger isn't automatically cheaper. Bulk packs usually — not always — win on a per-unit basis. It's still worth checking, since occasionally a mid-size pack is on sale and briefly beats the jumbo box.
  • Store brands are worth a real look. In our own samples, store-brand options (Amazon's own lines, wholesale-club brands) were consistently among the lowest per-unit prices in both categories, without a corresponding drop in size or count. Brand loyalty is a reasonable choice for fit or sensitivity reasons — just make it a deliberate one, not a default.

How to check the math yourself

Whenever you're unsure, or comparing something we haven't listed yet: take the total price, divide by the count (diapers) or by the ounces printed on the label (formula), and you have the same number we show on every listing. No app required — just annoying to do while standing in the aisle or thumbing through a phone, which is the actual problem this site solves.

Frequently asked questions

What is unit price and why does it matter for diapers and formula?

Unit price is the cost per individual item — per diaper, or per ounce of formula powder — instead of the total price of the package. It matters because pack sizes vary so much between brands that the total price alone doesn't tell you which option is actually cheaper to use.

How is the price per diaper calculated?

Total listing price divided by the number of diapers in the pack. A $44.94 pack of 144 diapers works out to $0.312 per diaper.

Why compare formula by ounces of powder instead of prepared bottles?

Because that's what the price on the container is actually based on. A given weight of powder makes several times that amount in ready-to-feed formula, and the mixing ratio shifts slightly by brand, so ounces of powder is the number that stays consistent and comparable across brands.

Is the biggest pack always the cheapest per diaper or per ounce?

Usually, but not always. It's still worth checking the actual number on each listing, since a mid-size pack on sale occasionally beats the jumbo size.

Are store-brand diapers and formula as good as name brands?

We only compare price, not medical or safety claims. On price, store-brand and wholesale-club options were consistently among the cheapest per-unit options in our listings without a corresponding cut in count or size. Fit and skin sensitivity are individual, so many parents still choose based on trial, not price alone.

What diaper size should I expect to need as my baby grows?

Diaper sizing is based on weight, not age, and varies by baby. Packaging lists the weight range directly (for example, Size 4 covers roughly 22-37 lbs), which is a better guide than age alone.
